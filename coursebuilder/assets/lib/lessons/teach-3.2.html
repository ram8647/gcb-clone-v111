    Learning Activities

    Hook/Motivation

    Discussion (5 minutes)

    Ask students if they have used the Snap Chat app before. Have a volunteer demonstrate how the app allows users to draw on the pictures. Then discuss as a class, or have students discuss in small groups, how the app might have been created. Ask questions such as:
    • What events cause things to happen when you use this app? [Sample answers: touching the screen, dragging a finger across the screen.]
    • What happens when each event occurs? [Sample answer: Dots and lines of color appear on the picture.]
    • What components are on the user interface? [Sample answer:There is a picture. There is also a set of buttons.]

    Formative assessment

    Listen to student conversations to assess their progresson (
    5.2.1
    and
    5.4.1
    ). Students should be able to identify at least one event that drives the SnapChap app and make reasonable conjectures as to how events trigger things that happen on the screen.

    Explanation

    Explain to students that in this lesson they will create an app that is similar to this part of SnapChat. However, this functionality is not new or unique to SnapChat; a paint program was one of the first programs developed to demonstrate the potential of personal computers.

    Experiences and Exploration

    Tutorial (30 minutes)

    Briefly review the App Inventor environment. Then have students complete the Paint Pot Tutorial. [
    P2
    ] Students may complete the tutorial independently using the video or written versions of the student lesson, or you may choose to lead the class through either version of the tutorial as students work in pairs to complete each step. As they work, encourage students to focus on the event-driven programming used in the app. In particular, call students’ attention to the questions under Event Driven Programming, just after the directions to add the Wipe button. Encourage students to think through the events that will need to be handled by the app and discuss their thoughts with a partner before moving on in the tutorial. [
    P3
    ]

    Discussion (5 minutes)

    Discuss the new elements introduced in the Paint Pot tutorial: setter blocks, touch events, and drag events. Have a few students describe each of these new elements in their own words. [
    P4
    ] Generate a class list of the events that drive the app and how each event is handled. Have students talk with partners about any aspects of the tutorial that they did not understand.

    Rethink, Reflect, and/or Revise

    Wrap up (5 minutes)

    Have students write a reflection in their portfolios to the following questions: [

    P1
    ]
    1. How many different types of events does this app respond to? Name each type of event.
    2. Describe the difference between the two Canvas events, Touched and Dragged.
    3. What do the X and Y properties represent in the Touched event handler?
    4. Describe the difference between the Start and Previous properties in the Dragged event handler.

    If time permits, suggest that students also try the interactive exercises for this lesson.

    Assessment Opportunties

    You can examine students’ work on the Paint Pot app, their reflection portfolio entries, and their performance on the interactive exercises to assess the progress on the following learning objectives. If students are able to do what is listed here, they are ready to move on to the next lesson.

    Paint Pot App

  • Learning Objective 1.2.1
    Students should be able to reproduce the functionality of the Paint Pot app on their own devices.
  • Learning Objective 5.5.1
    They should be able to use a number to define the radius of the paint dots.

    • Reflection entry in portfolio

  • Learning Objective 5.5.1
    Students should be able to describe all of the events used in the Paint Pot app (Click, Touch, and Drag).
  • Learning Objective 5.2.1
    In particular, they should mention that Touch events are brief and involve one location on a touchscreen, while Drag events involve sustained contact while moving a finger across the screen.
